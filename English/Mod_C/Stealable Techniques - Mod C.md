## Techniques You Can Steal From Each Text

| From this text... | Steal this technique... | Use it like this... |
|-------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **"Father and Child"** (Harwood) | Two-part structure + concrete physical imagery | Show a moment in childhood, then its echo in adulthood. Ground emotion in objects. |
| **"What Time Is It Now..."** (McCann) | Metafiction (the writer debates their choices) | Have your narrator pause mid-story to question what they're doing. "I could tell you about the tree, but that's not the story." |
| **"How to Marry Your Daughters"** (Garner) | Personal voice + humour + literary criticism blended | Write like you're talking to a smart friend. Mix observation with cultural commentary. Be funny about serious things. |
| **"Eulogy for Gough Whitlam"** (Pearson) | Accumulation + anaphora | Build momentum through repetition. "He was... he was... he was..." — accumulate qualities until the weight is undeniable. |
| **"The Lady of Shalott"** (Tennyson) | Symbolism + the mirror as structuring device | Use a single symbolic object (a mirror, a window, a photograph) to structure your entire piece. The object mediates between the character and the world. |

## Versatile Opening Lines Bank

Memorise 2-3 of these. Adapt them to ANY stimulus:

1. **In medias res (action):** *"The thing they don't tell you about [X] is that it happens slowly, then all at once."*
2. **Striking image:** *"The light came through the window like it always did — pale, patient, indifferent."*
3. **Memory trigger:** *"I don't remember the day, but I remember the light."*
4. **Question:** *"What do you do with a thing you can't undo?"*
5. **Specific detail:** *"The knife was in the drawer where he kept it, wrapped in a tea towel."*

## Skeleton Plan for an Imaginative Piece (Harwood-Inspired)

**Opening (in medias res):**
- Open with sensory detail grounded in physicality (Harwood-inspired)
- Establish speaker's worldview (naive, confident, or limited)
- Plant the moment the piece will reveal/undo

**Rising action:**
- Describe the target/objective
- Show the speaker's confidence
- Show the moment things start to go wrong
- Build toward the central realisation

**Turning point (key moment):**
- Use direct speech (a single line is enough)
- Echo Harwood's "End what you have begun" structure
- Let silence do the work

**Resolution / Echo:**
- Jump forward in time (the Harwood diptych move)
- The same speaker, older, looking back
- Show how this moment still lives in them

## Recommended Reflection Outline

1. **Purpose:** To explore how a single moment can shatter innocence and how that moment echoes across a lifetime
2. **Influence (Harwood):** Her two-part structure in "Father and Child" inspired my temporal shift; her use of "loose straw tangling in bowels" taught me to ground horror in physical detail rather than abstraction
3. **Techniques used:** In medias res, direct speech as turning point, sensory imagery, temporal shift
4. **Evaluation:** The direct speech "..." mirrors Harwood's "End what you have begun" — both achieve maximum emotional impact with minimal words
