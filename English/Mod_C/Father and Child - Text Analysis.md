## Full Structural Reading

### Part I: Barn Owl — The Loss of Innocence

The poem opens with a child sneaking out at dawn with their father's gun. The speaker calls themselves a "horny fiend" and "master of life and death" — delusions of godlike power.

The shooting is not clean: the owl is "ruined, beating his only wing", a "wrecked thing" that "hobbled in its own blood". The reality of death is messy, violent, awful — not the clean abstraction the child imagined.

The father arrives and delivers the gut-wrenching command: **"End what you have begun."**

This is not cruelty — it's a brutal lesson in responsibility. The child fires again, and the owl's "blank eyes shone once into mine, and slept". The final image of the child "owl blind in early sun" shows the child has been fundamentally changed — they now see the world differently.

### Part II: Nightfall — Reconciliation and Mortality

Forty years later. The father is now 80, "stick-thin", using a white stick. The roles have reversed — the child now leads. The walk is quiet, filled with small observations: "birds crown in flowering trees", "far distant suburbs shine with great simplicities".

The line *"Let us walk for this hour / as if death had no power / or were no more than sleep"* echoes back to the owl who "slept" after being shot. The father, like the owl, is approaching that same sleep.

The final stanza is devastating: the father speaks, his voice "near breaking point", and the grown child sees him as "old king, your marvellous journey's done". The poem ends with the recognition that some sorrows "no words, no tears can mend" — but the walk, the naming of things, the presence together — that is enough.

## Key Techniques You Can Use In Your Own Writing

| Technique | How Harwood does it | How YOU can use it |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Two-part structure** | Past / Present — the same speaker 40 years apart | Use a diptych structure: show a moment of impact, then its echo years later |
| **Shift in voice** | Child: arrogant, then horrified. Adult: tender, elegiac | Change register to show character growth across time |
| **Concrete imagery** | "loose straw tangling in bowels," "owl blind in early sun" | Ground big emotions in physical details — don't say "I was sad," show the wreckage |
| **Circularity** | "sleep" (owl dying) → "sleep" (death as sleep, Part II) | Echo an image from the opening at the end — shows how the past reverberates |
| **Direct speech** | "End what you have begun" — 5 words that change everything | Use a single line of dialogue as the turning point |
| **Silence / what's not said** | The child never speaks in Part II — only the father | Let silence do the work — what characters DON'T say is powerful |

## Key Quotes (close analysis)

### The arrogance of innocence
> *"I was a horny fiend, / a child who believed death clean and final, not this obscene / bundle of stuff that dropped, and dribbled through loose straw / tangling in bowels..."*

- **Technique:** Juxtaposition of mythic self-image ("horny fiend," "master of life and death") with brutal physical reality ("obscene bundle," "loose straw tangling in bowels")
- **Effect:** The gap between what the speaker EXPECTED and what ACTUALLY happened is the engine of the poem.
- **Use:** Ground big emotions in physical, even ugly, detail.

### Direct speech as turning point
> The father: *"End what you have begun."*

- **Technique:** Five words. No anger. No explanation. Just a command that changes everything.
- **Effect:** The power is in what's NOT said — the father doesn't lecture, doesn't punish. He forces the child to take responsibility.
- **Use:** A single line of dialogue can pivot your entire piece.

### Circular imagery (the "sleep" echo)
> Part I: The owl's "eyes / shone once into mine, and slept."
> Part II: "Let us walk for this hour / as if death had no power / or were no more than sleep."

- **Technique:** "Sleep" = death in Part I (the dying owl). "Sleep" returns in Part II (death as sleep, approaching the father).
- **Effect:** The echo across forty years shows how the past reverberates into the present.
- **Use:** Echo an image from your opening at your closing. It doesn't have to be the SAME image — it can be transformed by time.

## Thesis Statements for the Reflection

> *Drawing on Harwood's use of the two-part structure to contrast childhood arrogance with adult wisdom, I crafted my own piece to explore how a single violent moment can reverberate across a lifetime.*

> *Harwood's technique of grounding profound emotional truths in concrete physical detail — the "loose straw tangling in bowels" — inspired me to avoid abstraction and instead locate my character's grief in the material world they inhabit.*

> *By adopting Harwood's shift in voice between Parts I and II — from the naive bravado of "master of life and death" to the quiet reverence of "old king" — I sought to dramatise how experience reshapes our understanding of past events.*
