# Collection Analysis — Module C Prescribed Texts (How They Work Together)

> Module C is unique among your modules: the prescribed texts come as a **COLLECTION** — different forms (poetry, prose fiction, nonfiction, speech) that you study together to inform your own writing. This is the in-collection analysis — showing how the texts cohere as a teaching set about the craft of writing.

---

## The Collection's Overarching Argument

The Module C prescribed texts are a curated set asking a single question: **What does it mean to write well?**

Each text models a different craft choice:
- Some teach structure (Harwood's diptych, Tennyson's symbol)
- Some teach voice (Garner's intimacy, Pearson's political passion)
- Some teach the writer's relationship to the reader (McCann's metafiction)

Weaving these together, the collection says: **good writing is CONSCIOUS writing** — every choice is a craft choice, and the writer is accountable for it.

---

## How the Prescribed Texts Cohere

### 1. The Form Spectrum (variety as the spine of the collection)

The Module C texts cover FOUR distinct forms:

| Form | Text | Author |
|------|------|--------|
| Poetry | "Father and Child" | Gwen Harwood |
| Prose Fiction | "What Time Is It Now, Where You Are?" | Colum McCann |
| Nonfiction | "How to Marry Your Daughters" | Helen Garner |
| Speech | "Eulogy for Gough Whitlam" | Noel Pearson |
| Poetry | "The Lady of Shalott" | Alfred Lord Tennyson |

**Textual integrity argument:** The collection's coherence comes from VARIETY in form, all serving the same purpose — showing you different ways craft can be exercised. Read as a collection, they model the range of choices available to you as a writer.

### 2. The Craft Spectrum (what each text teaches)

| Text | Primary Craft Lesson |
|------|---------------------|
| **Harwood** "Father and Child" | Concrete physical imagery + temporal structure + direct speech |
| **McCann** "What Time Is It Now..." | Metafiction (writing-about-writing) + second person address |
| **Garner** "How to Marry Your Daughters" | Personal voice + humour + blending literary criticism with memoir |
| **Pearson** "Eulogy for Gough Whitlam" | Anaphora + accumulation + political passion through personal testimony |
| **Tennyson** "The Lady of Shalott" | Symbol + myth + a single structuring object (the mirror) |

**Textual integrity argument:** Each prescribed text teaches one or two craft moves vividly. Together they form a toolkit. The collection's argument is — you are not choosing between these styles; you are choosing which COMBINATION fits your piece.

### 3. The Recurring Concern (the writer's responsibility)

Across all five texts, a single recurring concern: **the writer's accountability to the reader**.

- **Harwood** demonstrates it through restraint (5 words "End what you have begun")
- **McCann** demonstrates it through self-exposure (the writer admits their choices mid-narrative)
- **Garner** demonstrates it through honesty (the personal essay blurs the line between confession and craft)
- **Pearson** demonstrates it through anaphora (each repetition is a vow to the dead)
- **Tennyson** demonstrates it through symbol (a single object bears the weight of the whole poem)

**Textual integrity argument:** The collection coheres around what might be called THE WRITER'S ETHICS — that every word is a choice, and worthy writing makes its choices consciously. This ethics shows up DIFFERENTLY in each form, but the principle is constant.

## How To Use the Collection In Your Essay / Reflection

### Step 1: Pick a primary text
Choose the text whose technique most clearly matches what you're doing.
- Imaginative piece about loss, childhood, transformation → **Harwood**
- Imaginative piece about writing itself → **McCann**
- Discursive / personal essay → **Garner**
- Persuasive speech → **Pearson**
- Imaginative piece built on a single symbolic object → **Tennyson**

### Step 2: Reference one backup text
NESA wants you to "draw on the stylistic features of ONE of your prescribed texts." One is sufficient, but referencing a second lifts you to Band 6 by showing breadth.

### Step 3: Name the technique PRECISELY
Vague: "I used imagery from Harwood."
Specific: "I drew on Harwood's technique of grounding emotional weight in concrete physical object — as in 'End what you have begun' — by centring my own piece on a small symbolic object that carries the narrative's meaning."

### Step 4: Show ADAPTATION, not copying
The marker wants to see that you UNDERSTOOD the technique and adapted it to a new context. Make this explicit.
- "While Harwood's father-figure uses the imperative, my piece uses [different move] because [contextual reason]."

## Sample Reflection (Framework — Generic)

> My purpose in my piece was to [purpose]. I drew primarily on the techniques of [PRIMARY PRESCRIBED TEXT], specifically [technique with quote]. I also referenced [SECONDARY PRESCRIBED TEXT] for [technique]. The adaptation of these techniques to my context required [what you changed and why]. I believe this demonstrates my understanding of [broader insight about craft].
