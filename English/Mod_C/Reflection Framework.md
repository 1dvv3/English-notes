# Reflection Framework — Part B (Module C)

> Use this 4-part formula for every Part B response in craft of writing. Targets Band 6.

---

## The Formula

### 1. PURPOSE (1-2 sentences)
**Question:** What were you trying to achieve?

**Template:**
> *"I sought to explore [theme] by [form/genre choice]. My piece aimed to [effect on audience]."*

**Examples:**
- *"I sought to explore how a single childhood moment can fundamentally alter one's understanding of the world — the shift from innocence to a more complex awareness."*
- *"I sought to dramatise the way grief reshapes a relationship by structuring the piece across two time zones."*
- *"I sought to use the conventions of the personal essay to examine [theme] through the lens of [specific experience]."*

**Band 6 move:** Pair purpose with FORM. "I sought to dramatise [theme] by [strapping/grafting it to a specific form (diptych, monologue, letter-in-fragments)] — because the form itself models [what the content is about]."

---

### 2. INFLUENCES (2-3 sentences)
**Question:** How did your prescribed text(s) inspire your choices?

**CRITICAL RULES:**
- **NAME** the prescribed text (e.g., Gwen Harwood's "Father and Child")
- **NAMETWO** specific techniques (one is fine but two lifts you)
- **EMBED A QUOTE** — even half a line ("'loose straw tangling in bowels'") to prove you know the text
- **EXPLAIN** how you adapted it to your context (not copied it)
- **SHOW ADAPTATION, NOT COPYING** — make the change-to-context explicit

**Template:**
> *"My prescribed text, [NAME], significantly influenced my choices. Specifically, [author]'s use of [technique 1] — as in '[brief quote]' — inspired me to [your adaptation]. I applied this by [concrete description of what you did]; however, I adapted the technique to my contemporary context by [specific change]."*

**Example (Harwood-inspired, Band 6):**
> *"My prescribed text, Gwen Harwood's 'Father and Child,' significantly influenced my choices. Harwood's two-part structure — juxtaposing a childhood act of violence ('Barn Owl') with a reflective adult perspective forty years later ('Nightfall') — inspired me to frame my own piece as a temporal shift. I applied this by structuring my opening in present-tense childhood memory and my concluding paragraphs in reflective adult voice. However, I adapted the technique to a contemporary context by collapsing the time gap from forty years to a single afternoon of therapy — the 'forty years' of psychological distance had to become, for a young adult, a single session of forced reflection."*

---

### 3. TECHNIQUES (2-3 sentences)
**Question:** What techniques did you use and WHY?

**CRITICAL RULES:**
- Name 2-3 specific techniques
- For each: what was the intended EFFECT on the reader?
- Link each technique back to PURPOSE

**Template:**
> *"I employed [technique 1] to create [intended effect]; [technique 2] to [intended effect]; and [technique 3] to [intended effect]. Each technique functioned to [purpose/sustained coherence]."*

**Example (Band 6):**
> *"I employed 'in medias res' to create immediate immersion — opening on the precise moment of breakage rather than its parenthesised aftermath; concrete sensory imagery ('the knife, wrapped in a tea towel, the blade worn thin') to anchor abstract grief in the material world and refuse the temptation of telling rather than showing; and a single line of direct speech as a structural pivot — echoing Harwood's 'End what you have begun' with my own five-word command in the moment of revelation. Together, these techniques functioned to embody my purpose of dramatising how a single moment's violence reverberates long after the act itself has passed."*

---

### 4. EVALUATION (2 sentences)
**Question:** Were you successful? What would you change?

**CRITICAL RULES:**
- Be honest but not negative
- Be SPECIFIC — name the SUCCESS and the REFINEMENT
- Mention what you would refine AND WHY it matters
- Show metacognition (knowing about your own craft)

**Template:**
> *"I believe my piece successfully [specific achievement], because [specific evidence]. Given more time, I would refine [specific element] — [why this matters]."*

**Example (Band 6):**
> *"I believe my piece successfully translated Harwood's approach to a contemporary, personal context while maintaining her emphasis on the way formative experiences continue to inhabit an adult's consciousness. The opening image — the child gripping a 'tea towel' her mother did not wrap well — establishes the inheritance of damage that the rest of the piece explores. Given more time, however, I would refine the pacing of the temporal shift in the closing paragraph — the present-tense moment risks feeling rushed against the more sustained childhood scene, and a more deliberate break between the time zones would protect the emotional weight of each."*

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Vague influences ("I was inspired by Harwood") | Name the SPECIFIC technique + embed a brief quote from the text |
| Listing techniques without effect | Always pair with the EFFECT on the reader |
| No evaluation / evaluation without specifics | State one concrete achievement AND one specific refinement |
| Mentioning the text but not engaging with it | Show that you UNDERSTOOD the technique — why does it work in the original? |
| Repeating your Part A story in Part B | Part B is META — talk about the CRAFT, not the narrative |
| Forgetting to embed a quote from the prescribed text | A 2-3 word quote is enough — it proves real engagement |
| Treating Part B as an apology for not being a great writer | Reflection is INDEPENDENTLY marked — it can be Band 6 even if creative is Band 4 |

## SK Sample Reflection — Generic

> My purpose in writing [piece title] was to [purpose]. I chose the [form] form to allow the reader to experience [what the form enables]. My prescribed text, [AUTHOR]'s "[TITLE]", significantly influenced my choices. Specifically, [author]'s use of [technique with brief quote] inspired me to [your adaptation]. I applied this by [concrete description]. I also referenced [SECONDARY PRESCRIBED TEXT] for [technique]. I employed [technique 1] to [effect], [technique 2] to [effect], and [technique 3] to [effect]. Each technique was chosen because [brief reason linking to your purpose]. I believe my piece successfully [specific achievement], though I would refine [specific refinement] with further drafting. Overall, the piece demonstrates my developing understanding of craft as conscious choice — every word a decision, every structure a stance.

## Worked Sample Reflection (Band 6 — Harwood-inspired)

*Below is a fully built sample that would score in the top band. Memorise its STRUCTURE, separate from its content — you can adapt to your own piece.*

---

**Sample Creative Title:** *The Garden After*

**Stimulus:** Compose a piece of imaginative writing that explores how a single loss can permanently alter a relationship with the natural world.

**Sample Reflection:**

> My purpose in writing "The Garden After" was to dramatise how the death of a parent persists in the strangest material of the surviving child's life — how loss reorganises the most ordinary landscapes. I chose to frame the piece as a 36-hour unfolding of a single young adult's return to a deceased father's garden, structuring the narrative so that the gradual detection of small sabotages — a hose left running, a fork misplaced in a wrong drawer — accumulates where dialogue cannot. The form, a single extended present-tense afternoon, was chosen because the lingering of grief in objects is itself a form of registering time differently — and present tense enacts this rather than reporting it.
>
> My prescribed text, Gwen Harwood's "Father and Child," significantly influenced my choices. Harwood's two-part diptych structures — juxtaposing the child's dawn act of violence in "Barn Owl" with the adult's drawn-out vigil in "Nightfall" — inspired my own juxtaposition of two time-zones within a single setting: the child's remembered handling of the father's tools, and the adult speaker's later quiet naming of those same tools. Specifically, Harwood's anchoring of enormous grief in concrete physical detail — "loose straw tangling in bowels," "owl blind in early sun" — taught me to refuse abstraction and to instead locate my piece's emotion in the small, weighted, material world the dead father organised. I applied this by centring my piece on three precise objects — a pair of gloves, a returned library book, a kettle scoured to the point of ruin — each carrying the unsaid weight. I adapted the technique to my contemporary context by collapsing Harwood's forty-year time gap into a 36-hour visit; the long perspective of a child growing into an adult is, for a young-adult speaker, the long afternoon of returning to a house where the parent is conspicuously absent.
>
> I employed three specific techniques. First, present-tense opening ("The letterbox key is where he left it.") — to register immediately that the speaker is performing a return, not living inside the home; second, what I think of as object-catalogue enumeration (each tool, drawer, plant — described as if the dead father might yet check on them) — to slow the reader's eye and refuse them the privilege of skipping the in-between; third, deliberate restraint at the period of maximum grief (the speaker turns away from the father's workbench mid-sentence, without finishing) — to deliver emotional weight through absence rather than declaration. Each technique functioned to embody my purpose: the form's refusal of resolution enacts the speaker's refusal of the closure he keeps walking towards.
>
> I believe my piece successfully translates Harwood's approach to a contemporary personal context while maintaining her emphasis on the way grief organises an adult's relation to inherited objects. The single most effective choice, I think, was the scoured kettle — it is the smallest, silliest detail that does the most work, because it suggests a parent who cared about objects so specifically that the surviving child now feels duty-bound to validate them, and the validation is itself the mourning. Given further drafting, I would refine the rhythm of the present-tense opening across the first two paragraphs. The opening sentence is strong, but the second paragraph risks slipping into explanatory past tense before the pattern has settled; a more consistent opening would let the present-tense texture do more of the piece's initial work, before the memories emerge as disruption.

---

**Take the following from this sample:**
1. Each paragraph of the reflection has a CLEAR job (purpose stated, influence argued, technique justified, evaluation offered).
2. The reflection uses the prescribed text SPECIFICALLY — Harwood by name, two specific techniques, a verbatim quote, and an explicit change made for the speaker's contemporary context.
3. The reflection does NOT apologise. It does not say "I'm not sure if this works" — it asserts what works and what would be improved, both with reasons.
4. The reflection does not retell the creative piece. It discusses CRAFT.
