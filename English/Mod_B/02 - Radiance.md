# 2. Radiance — Analysis

> **Collection:** David Malouf, *Earth Hour* (2014)

---

## What It's About

"Radiance" catalogues the WAYS vision comes to people — not everyone, but some. Some get stillness, some get a shadow at noon, some get a wound that is the gift. When they die, the silence they leave continues to "speak" beside the living — in a bowl, in a book, as a "companionable cloud" / angel at one's side.

The poem wonders about the afterlife of attention. The dead leave behind a kind of weather.

---

## Verbatim Text (excerpt)

> *"Not all come to it / but some do, and serenely. // No saying / what party they are of / or what totem / animal walks with them."*

> *"For some a wound, some / a gift; and for some / the wound is the gift."*

> *"When they too become one / of the Grateful Dead, it is / the silence they leave, / in a bowl, in a book, / that speaks and may join us; // its presence, / waist high at our side, / a commotion, a companionable / cloud with the shape and smell of / an unknown familiar, call it / an angel..."*

---

## Key Quotes (close analysis)

### Quote 1 — "Not all come to it / but some do, and serenely"
- **Technique:** Antithesis + first-line anonymity + paradoxical adverb ("but some do, and serenely" — and yet they're calm about it)
- **Effect:** The poem starts by NEITHER universalising NOR limiting. It opens a question that the rest of the poem keeps answering in different cases. "Not all" implies a category. "But some do" salvages the reader.

### Quote 2 — "For some a wound, some / a gift; and for some / the wound is the gift"
- **Technique:** Anaphora ("For some") + chiastic sentence structure (wound/gift → gift/wound, the second version identical except reversed)
- **Effect:** The reversal IS the insight. Repetition enacts fusion. The wound can become the gift. A reader cannot parse the line without feeling the word order shift.

### Quote 3 — "the silence they leave, / in a bowl, in a book, / that speaks"
- **Technique:** Personification (silence as speaker) + concrete enumeration ("a bowl, in a book") the abstract codified into objects
- **Effect:** The dead leave silence — but silence, here, is a thing. Not nothing. The list of where the silence lives (bowl, book) anchors the abstract to the tactile, and personification makes silence a companion rather than an absence.

### Quote 4 — "call it / an angel"
- **Technique:** Parenthetical instruction ("call it") + the salting of a religious term in a colloquial grip + precise endpoints of the line
- **Effect:** Malouf doesn't TELL you the presence is an angel — he makes you NAME it one. The reader becomes complicit in the metaphysics. The angel is invoked, not asserted.

## Techniques to Spot

| Technique | How Malouf does it | Effect |
|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| **Cataloguing / accumulation** | A litany of "for some / for some / for some" | Builds rhythm that becomes ritual — like a prayer |
| **Personification of the abstract** | "the silence they leave... that speaks" | The dead persist as presence, not as memory |
| **Anaphora** | "For some" repeated 4+ times | Litany structure mimics a hymn or a quiet rosary |
| **List as metaphysical move** | "a bowl, in a book" — places where silence lives | Concretises what would otherwise be only abstract — gives the dead a homely address |
| **Tone: conversational intimacy** | "call it / an angel" | Reader inside the lyric — almost Malouf speaking to you |

## How It Links to Other Poems

| Pair | Thematic linkage |
|------|------------------|
| **Radiance ↔ A Recollection of Starlings** | Both work through moments of unprecedented vision that arrive unbidden. Starlings = the writing-imagination as accidental flock. Radiance = the angel-haunting as accidental luminosity. Both versions of grace as an event. |
| **Radiance ↔ Aquarius II** | Both pivot on GRATITUDE — Radiance catalogues the gift as wound/wound-as-gift; Aquarius II thanks the world for the privilege of being alive in it. They sit at opposite ends of an arc that's all about recognising. |
| **Radiance ↔ Earth Hour (title)** | Both poems imagine a different relationship to darkness — Radiance's angel/companion lets the speaker enter "beyond / silence"; the Earth Hour title poem constructs darkness as a moment of cosmic solidarity. |

---

## Thesis Fragment

> *"Radiance" catalogues, rather than describes, the arrival of insight. Malouf refuses to universalise — there is no single way vision comes — but the accumulation itself enacts the poem's claim: vision arrives, for those it comes to, as a presence, an angel, a kind of weather. The poem is a rosary in free verse.*
