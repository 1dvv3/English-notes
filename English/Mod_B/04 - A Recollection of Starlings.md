# 4. A Recollection of Starlings: Rome '84 — Analysis

> **Collection:** David Malouf, *Earth Hour* (2014)
> **Note:** Malouf's Earth Hour booklets sometimes include this poem; check your school's prescribed list. The analysis below is based on widely published scholarly reading.

---

## What It's About

A remembered vision of starlings in flight — Rome 1984. The flock moves as if it were a single writing hand: wheeling, ballooning, stooping, breaking, regrouping. Malouf reads the flock as an IMAGE for how poems, how consciousness itself, come together: small independent pieces, in the moment they connect, creating something bigger than any individual bird or word.

---

## Key Ideas / Themes

| Theme | How it surfaces |
|-------|----------------|
| Nature as the model for poetic/cultural production | The starlings aren't just a pretty sight — they ARE the activity of imagination externalised |
| Memory as a transport | "Recollection" placed in the title — what is preserved isn't the birds but the act of seeing them, refracted through 40 years |
| Collective vs individual | Each bird is a unit; the murmuration IS the phenomenon — a comment on how individual lines come together into a poem |
| Pattern and dissolution | The flock both makes and unmakes patterns. Form is provisional; the moment the pattern peaks it breaks into the next. |
| Rome as a place of layered time | 1984 + Starlings at Ponte Sant'Angelo + ancient/Christian/Renaissance geography — all haunt the speaking voice |

---

## Key Techniques to Spot

| Technique | How it works |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Long enjambed lines, no end punctuation** | The poem simulates the unbroken flow of the flock, refusing the stop-and-rest of traditional form |
| **Repetition of present participles** | "spinning", "wheeling", "bridging", "breaking", "ballooning", "stooping", "swaying" — the verbs are themselves the flock |
| **Absence of punctuation at line ends** | Unlike most of Malouf's poems, this one signals formal disruption — the lineation itself becomes the murmuration |
| **Comparison without explicit marker** | The poem builds toward the analogy (starlings : writing / starlings : gathering of mind) but resists "this is like that" — it lets you SEE it rather than be told |
| **Place / temporal doubling** | The Rome of 1984 is also the Rome of childhood / of inherited cultural memory — multiple Romes at once |

## Thesis Fragment

> *"A Recollection of Starlings: Rome '84" uses the natural phenomenon of murmuration as both subject and method. The poem's enjambed, unpunctuated flow enacts the unbroken wheeling of the flock, while its accumulating present participles ("spinning and wheeling, / bridging and breaking") mime the continuous reshape of which murmuration consists. The analogy Malouf builds is precisely to the activity of writing: each bird is a word, every flock is a poem, and the structural innovation is the refusal of fixity — every pattern dissolves into the next.*
