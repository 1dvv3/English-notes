# Common Module — Texts and Human Experiences

> **Module:** Texts and Human Experiences (Paper 1, Section II — 20 marks, ~50 min)
> **Prescribed Text:** William Shakespeare, *The Merchant of Venice* (c. 1596-98)

---

## What NESA Wants

This module is about how a text — through its language, form, and structure — **ignites ideas about collective human experiences** that enrich our view of the world.

The module is built on TWO paired rubric terms:

| Rubric Term | What it Demands |
|-------------|----------------|
| **"Ignite ideas"** | The text SPARKS new thinking — it doesn't just describe, it PROVOKES |
| **"Collective human experiences"** | Shared, not individual — prejudice, grief, love, justice, mortality, transformation |
| **"Enrich our view"** | Deepen, complicate, or change how we see — NEVER just confirm what you already know |
| **"To what extent"** | You AGREE but can acknowledge LIMITS or caveats — strong agreement with nuance, not blanket endorsement |

The title phrase "Texts and Human Experiences" frames the entire module: texts don't just RECORD human experience — they actively SHAPE how we understand it.

## The 2023 HSC Question (YOU WILL BEAT IT)

> *"A text can ignite ideas about collective human experiences that enrich our view of the world."*
> To what extent do you agree with this statement in relation to your prescribed text.

This is the most likely question type. Memorise the keyword breakdown:

| Keyword | What the marker wants |

| "ignite ideas" | The text SPARKS new thinking — doesn't just describe, it PROVOKES |

## Recommended Thesis Angle

*STRONGLY AGREE* — Shakespeare's play enriches our understanding by REFUSING easy answers. The text ignites ideas about:
- **Prejudice** as a collective (not individual) failing — we are all complicit
- **Justice** as indistinguishable from revenge when stripped of mercy
- **Love** as inseparable from economics — we say love is priceless, but we constantly price it

BUT with one limitation: Shakespeare's 1590s frame means some voices are filtered through a Christian majority — Shylock's "Hath not a Jew eyes?" is asked but never answered. The play ignites the idea of marginalisation WITHOUT giving the marginalised full expression.

---

## Sample Thesis (Band 6)

> *Through the relentless irony of a Christian society that preaches mercy and practises revenge, Shakespeare's The Merchant of Venice ignites ideas about the collective human experience of complicity — exposing how prejudice is not the act of an individual villain but a structural feature of communities who refuse to recognise shared humanity. Yet the play enriches our view only up to a point: in encoding Shylock's humanity without ever validating it, Shakespeare's text refuses the very closure that would make its compassion complete.*

## How to Structure a Common Mod Essay

**Intro (4-5 sentences):**
1. Hook — broad statement about the human experience being explored (prejudice, mercy, etc.)
2. Introduce text: title, author, form, year
3. State thesis — your argument about what the text reveals about human experiences
4. Flag the complication — show nuance from the start (Band 6 move)
5. Outline — briefly mention the 2-3 ideas/techniques you'll explore

**Body paragraphs (3, ~9-10 sentences each):**
1. **Topic sentence** — idea linking to thesis + technique
2. **Embedded quote** — woven into your sentence, NOT dropped
3. **Analysis** — technique + effect + what does this REVEAL about human experience?
4. **ENRICHMENT step** — how does this *ignite ideas* / *complicate* / *challenge* assumption?
5. **Link back** to thesis

**Conclusion (4 sentences):**
1. Restate thesis (reworded, deepened)
2. Summarise key insights
3. Name the LIMITATION — what the text can't do
4. Final statement — broader truth about being human

**Key for Band 6:** Engage with the QUESTION verbatim. Drop "ignite ideas," "collective human experience," "enrich our view" exactly into your analysis — the markers look for these.
