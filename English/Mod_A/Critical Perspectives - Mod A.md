## Key Critics & Their Ideas

### Virginia Woolf's own essay "Modern Fiction" (1919/1925)
- **Key idea:** "Examine for a moment an ordinary mind on an ordinary day." The novel should capture consciousness as it is actually lived — fragmented, associative, layered — not as a tidy chronological plot.
- **How to use:** Form justification. Why does Woolf use interior monologue? Why does Septimus's POV exist in fragmented bursts? Because that's what experience LOOKS LIKE. Woolf's modernism is a CRAFT choice, not just a style.
- **Example deployment:** "As Woolf herself argued in 'Modern Fiction,' the novel should follow consciousness, not chronology. Her choice to intercut Clarissa's day with Septimus's war trauma is thus not narrative whim but method — the form enacts an argument about how memory and trauma intrude on present experience."

### Michael Cunningham (novelist of *The Hours*, 1998)
- **Key idea:** His novel was an explicit homage to Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* — he wanted to ask "Who is the Mrs Dalloway of today?" The 2002 film is an extension of his project, not a betrayal of it.
- **How to use:** Daldry's decisions flow from Cunningham's literary conceit. Three timelines → three generations of women → one argument about the persistence of patterns of female experience.
- **Example deployment:** "As Cunningham has acknowledged, *The Hours* was conceived as a deliberate palimpsest — writing-against rather than writing-after Woolf's novel. Daldry's film, by extension, treats the 1925 text not as historical artefact but living source."

### Toril Moi (*Sexual/Textual Politics*, 1985)
- **Key idea:** Modernist writers like Woolf were caught between feminist consciousness and aesthetic conservatism — they represented women's inner lives but often refused to engage directly with lesbian desire as a POLITICAL identity.
- **How to use:** Why is the Sally kiss wrapped up, not named? Because Woolf's modernism valued ambiguity over political statement. This explains the encoding. It also explains why Daldry might be read as either LIBERATING Woolf's implicit politics or REPLACING them with a more explicit, less ambiguous politics.
- **Example deployment:** "Toril Moi's analysis of modernist ambivalence illuminates Woolf's handling of the Sally kiss: the 'revelation' is named but not identified, protecting the moment from ideology. Daldry's decision to make three explicit kisses across three timelines risks reading this unwrapping as PROGRESS — but is visibility the same as liberation?"

### Heather Love (*Feeling Backward*, 2007)
- **Key idea:** LGBTQ+ history is a history of LOSS rather than progress. The past was not a "wrong" version of the present; it produced specific forms of knowledge (encoded intimacy, sanctioned silences) that the present has lost.
- **How to use:** REVERSE the easy reading. Daldry's explicitness is not automatically "better" than Woolf's encoding. Each has its costs. Woolf's wrapped gift holds a kind of meaning the unwrapped kiss cannot hold.
- **Example deployment:** "Heather Love's work on queer history as a history of loss reframes the Woolf/Daldry conversation: the wrapped kiss of 1925 encodes a particular intimacy that 2002's open kiss cannot access. The conversation is not a story of progressive liberation but of trade-offs in what can be felt, what can be said."

### David Edgar (*State of Play*, 2013 — on contemporary theatre/film)
- **Key idea:** Post-postmodern art struggles with sincerity. After a century of irony, second-guessing, and metafictional quotation, contemporary works are trying to find ways back to genuine feeling without naïveté.
- **How to use:** Daldry's film lives in this tension. It KNOWS it is quoting Woolf (metafictional), but it wants the audience's tears (sincere). This contradiction is the film itself.
- **Example deployment:** "As David Edgar has argued of contemporary art more broadly, *The Hours* is caught between citation and sincerity — quoting Woolf with full awareness that it does so, while asking its audience to feel Septimus's suicide as Septimus's suicide rather than as literary homage."

### Elaine Showalter (on Septimus)
- **Key idea:** Septimus is the modern novel's first major shell-shock victim. He prefigures Hemingway, Ford Madox Ford, and the entire British trauma novel. Woolf was using the novel to articulate something the medical profession refused to name.
- **How to use:** For the medical-establishment-as-tyrant reading. Holmes and Bradshaw are villains, not just doctors. They represent the social insistence on conformity over expression.
- **Example deployment:** "Elaine Showalter's reading of Septimus as the modern novel's originating trauma figure illuminates why his voice in *Mrs Dalloway* is dispersed across fragmented interior monologue — Woolf is not simply characterising a man but inventing a literary form adequate to psychic damage."

## How to Integrate Critics Into Your Essay

**Formula:** Quote/paraphrase the critic → connect to thesis → show HOW the critic's idea illuminates your textual evidence.

**TEMPLATE 1 (one-critic approach):**
> [Critic's name] argues that [their thesis]. This reading illuminates [scene/quote] where [text] does [what the critic describes]. Far from [common reading], the text actually [deeper meaning]...

**TEMPLATE 2 (build a conversation between critics):**
> Where [Critic A] sees [interpretation A], [Critic B] pushes further by suggesting [interpretation B]. The tension between these readings is itself productive, because [insight the tension reveals about the texts]...

**CUNNING MOVE:** Use two critics who DISAGREE. This shows depth. For example:
- Moi (Woolf's encoding is modernist restraint) vs Love (the encoding is loss) — both correct, both illuminating
- Show you've read the disagreement, not just chosen a side

**NB:** Always have ONE quote + ONE analysis from EACH TEXT. Critics are scaffolding, not substitutes for close reading.

## Exam-Type Question Keyword Bank

### "How does [later text] illuminate [earlier text]?"
Useful critics: **Cunningham** (the conversation is intentional, conceived), **Showalter** (Septimus precedent)

### "To what extent has context determine[d] the values of [texts]?"
Useful critics: **Woolf's "Modern Fiction"** (form follows consciousness), **Moi** (modernist ambivalence), **Showalter** (shell shock as social pressure)

### "Evaluate the significance of [an] aspect in contributing to the textual conversation."
Useful critics: **Love** (loss/progress framing) — the most distinctive model reader for evaluating THE CRUCIAL BALANCE

### "Consider the complexities of the textual conversation — does the later text extend, complicate, or critique the earlier?"
Useful critics: **Edgar** (post-postmodern sincerity), **Cunningham** (homage-as-method)
