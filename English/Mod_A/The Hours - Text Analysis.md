# The Hours — Text Analysis (Stephen Daldry, 2002)

> **Text 2 of Module A**
> **Form:** Postmodern film (adapted from Michael Cunningham's novel)
> **Context:** Filmed 2002 — post-9/11, post-AIDS-crisis

---

## The Big Picture

A film in THREE CONVERGING TIMELINES:
1. **Virginia Woolf, 1923** — writing Mrs Dalloway in Richmond, England
2. **Laura Brown, 1951** — a housewife in suburban Los Angeles reading Mrs Dalloway
3. **Clarissa Vaughan, 2001** — a New York editor planning a party for her dying ex-lover Richard

The film knows its audience knows the source text. It is metafictional — a film about a woman writing a novel about a day that the audience is watching unfold across three timelines.

---

## The Three Kisses (Daldry's Reimagining)

Daldry gives Woolf's single Sally kiss to THREE characters across three timelines:

### 1. Virginia Woolf (1923)
Kisses her sister Vanessa in a moment of desperate intimacy. *"I seem to be unraveling."* Vanessa's response: *"You're not unraveling."* The kiss is between blood relatives — Daldry makes queerness familial, ordinary, a form of care.

### 2. Laura Brown (1951)
Kisses her neighbour Kitty. Kitty has come to ask Laura to feed her cat while she's in hospital. The kiss happens in a kitchen, in daylight, after Kitty confesses she feels "lucky" to be losing the ability to have children. Laura kisses her — a moment of pure, unspoken recognition between two women who understand each other's trappedness. Kitty pulls away: *"You're sweet."* The moment is acknowledged and then brushed aside.

### 3. Clarissa Vaughan (2001)
No single climactic kiss — because she doesn't need one. She is openly in a relationship with Sally. The kiss has been NORMALISED. The absence of a "revelation" kiss in the 2001 timeline IS Daldry's point: in 2001, queer love doesn't need to be a wrapped gift. It can just... be.

**What Daldry TRANSFORMS:**
- Woolf's SINGLE kiss → Daldry's THREE kisses across 80 years
- Woolf's "wrapped up" metaphor → Daldry's unwrapped, visible, normalised intimacy
- Woolf's violent interruption → Daldry's gentle acknowledgments
- Woolf's one moment of "the most exquisite" → Daldry's ongoing, sustained queer relationships

## Richard Brown — Daldry's Septimus

**Who he is:** Clarissa Vaughan's former lover, a celebrated poet dying of AIDS. He is called "Richard" — not Septimus. This is Daldry's most significant transformation.

In Woolf, the suicide is a MINOR character (a stranger to Clarissa).
In Daldry, the suicide is Clarissa Vaughan's FORMER LOVER — central to her life.

**His death:**
Richard sits at the window. He tells Clarissa: *"I've stayed alive for you. But now I have to go."* He has lived for her, for her parties, for the life she creates around him. But he is exhausted. He slides from the window. Clarissa watches him fall.

**What Daldry transforms:**
- Septimus (stranger, abstraction) → Richard (lover, intimate)
- Septimus escapes Dr. Holmes → Richard escapes the party ("the party, the noise")
- Septimus's death is a VICTORY → Richard's death is an EXHAUSTION
- Clarissa hears about Septimus → Clarissa Vaughan WATCHES Richard die

---

## "Someone has to die" — The Metafictional Move

In one scene, Woolf (Kidman) is writing Mrs Dalloway and says:

> *"Someone has to die so that the rest of us should value life more."*

This is Daldry's thesis. The film says aloud what the novel encoded. Woolf had Septimus die so that Clarissa could choose life. Daldry has Richard die so that his Clarissa can choose life. And — metafictionally — WOOLF HERSELF will die, by suicide, in 1941. The film knows this. The audience knows this. Every scene with Kidman's Woolf is shadowed by her eventual death.

---

## The Opening Line

> *"I'm going to buy the flowers myself."*

Compare to Woolf's *"Mrs Dalloway said she would buy the flowers herself."*

**Resonance:** Both Clarissas assert agency through the smallest domestic act. The party, the flowers, the ritual — across 80 years, the woman's life is structured by the same gestures.

**Dissonance:** Woolf's third-person naming ("Mrs Dalloway") defines Clarissa by marriage. Daldry's first-person ("I'm going to") gives Clarissa Vaughan direct agency. But does the first person genuinely liberate her? Or is she trapped by the same ritual in a different context?

---

## The Party

The party DOESN'T happen. Richard kills himself at the start of the day. Clarissa spends the evening sitting with Laura Brown (Richard's mother), in the quiet aftermath.

**Resonance:** Both texts use the party as the STRUCTURING DEVICE. The day builds toward it. It gives the woman's life its shape.

**Dissonance:** Woolf's Clarissa gets her party. Daldry's Clarissa is denied it. In 2002, the ritual fails. The party as "covering the silence" is rejected. Daldry suggests that in a post-AIDS, post-9/11 world, the performance cannot hold.
