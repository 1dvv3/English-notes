# Module A — Textual Conversations

> **Module:** Textual Conversations (Paper 2, Section I — 20 marks, ~40 min)
> **Pair:** Virginia Woolf, *Mrs Dalloway* (1925) AND Stephen Daldry, *The Hours* (2002)

---

## What NESA Wants

The later text (The Hours, 2002) is in an active DIALOGUE with the earlier text (Mrs Dalloway, 1925). This is NOT a simple comparison.

| Rubric term | What it demands |
|-------------|----------------|
| **Resonance** | Where the texts ECHO each other — shared themes, techniques, images that persist |
| **Dissonance** | Where the texts DIVERGE — differences driven by changed context |
| **Reimagining** | Daldry ACTIVELY TRANSFORMS Woolf — he doesn't just borrow |
| **Values** | Each text EMBEDS the values of its context — 1925 post-WWI vs 2002 post-9/11 |
| **Context** | The social, historical, cultural conditions that SHAPE what a text can express |

## Quick Thesis Formula

> *In [The Hours], [Daldry] reimagines [Woolf's] exploration of [shared concern — female identity, queer desire, mental anguish] for a [postmodern/post-AIDS] context, [resonating with AND challenging] its modernist predecessor to reveal that [INSIGHT about how context shapes what can be spoken — and what remains unchanged across time].*

**Example thesis:**
> *Stephen Daldry's The Hours engages in a textual conversation with Virginia Woolf's Mrs Dalloway, both resonating with her modernist concern for the richness of the inner life while challenging her indirect encoding of queer desire and mental anguish. Through its three converging timelines and explicit metafictional framing, Daldry's postmodern film articulates what Woolf's 1925 novel could only encode — yet the conversation ultimately reveals that explicitness is not liberation: both Clarissas remain defined by the party, the performance, the ritual of domesticity that structures a woman's life across a century.*
