# Mrs Dalloway — Text Analysis (Virginia Woolf, 1925)

> **Text 1 of Module A**
> **Form:** Modernist novel
> **Context:** London, June 1923 (a single day) — published 1925, post-WWI

---

## The Big Picture

A day in the life of Clarissa Dalloway, 52, as she prepares for a party in post-WWI London. The novel moves through her consciousness — memory, regret, the texture of an ordinary day — across one June in 1923.

Beneath the surface: war trauma (Septimus), queer desire encoded (Clarissa + Sally), the failure of psychiatric "treatment," and the party as a fragile ritual against the void.

---

## The Sally Kiss — THE Key Scene

This scene is the most important single passage for Module A. Both texts centre on it. This is what you quote.

> *"Then came the most exquisite moment of her whole life passing a stone urn with flowers in it. Sally stopped; picked a flower; kissed her on the lips. The whole world might have turned upside down! The others disappeared; there she was alone with Sally. And she felt that she had been given a present, wrapped up, and told just to keep it, not to look at it — a diamond, something infinitely precious, wrapped up, which, as they walked (up and down, up and down), she uncovered, or the radiance burnt through, the revelation, the religious feeling! — when old Joseph and Peter faced them: 'Star-gazing?' said Peter. It was like running one's face against a granite wall in the darkness! It was shocking; it was horrible!"*

**Close analysis:**

- **"The most exquisite moment of her whole life"** — Clarissa, 52, remembers this as THE defining moment. Not her wedding. Not the birth of her child. A kiss with a girl at 18.
- **"The whole world might have turned upside down!"** — The exclamation mark is Woolf's signal: this is a moment of COSMIC significance wrapped in the most ordinary setting (a garden, a stone urn, a flower).
- **"A diamond, something infinitely precious, wrapped up"** — Clarissa cannot NAME what she received. It's wrapped, hidden. The metaphor IS the meaning: queerness in 1925 exists as a wrapped gift you cannot unwrap in public.
- **"The revelation, the religious feeling!"** — The language of religion applied to queer desire. This is not profane — it is SACRED. Woolf elevates the kiss to a spiritual experience.
- **"Star-gazing?"** — Peter's interruption is devastating. One word. He reduces the most profound moment of Clarissa's life to something banal, a joke.
- **"Like running one's face against a granite wall in the darkness!"** — the violence of the metaphor reveals how traumatic the interruption was. The wall is heterosexual society itself.
- **"It was shocking; it was horrible!"** — Not the kiss. The INTERRUPTION. What is shocking is that this moment could be so casually destroyed.

**What Woolf encoded:**
- Queerness exists but cannot be spoken
- The moment is sacred but private — "wrapped up"
- The outside world (Peter, Joseph, heterosexual normality) violently interrupts intimacy
- The memory persists for 34 years but the feeling cannot be recreated — "No, the words meant absolutely nothing to her now." The kiss is lost to time.

## Septimus Warren Smith — The War Wound

**Who he is:** A decorated WWI veteran, 30, suffering from what we now call PTSD. He married Rezia in Milan after the war. He hears birds singing in Greek (his dead friend Evans speaks through them). He believes he has "committed an appalling crime" — failing to feel grief for Evans's death.

**The doctors:**
- **Dr. Holmes:** The "repulsive brute." His prescription: "nothing whatever the matter with him." Holmes represents society's refusal to acknowledge invisible wounds.
- **Sir William Bradshaw:** Knighted, powerful. His prescription: "rest, rest, rest." His key words: **"proportion"** (everyone must conform) and **"conversion"** (the non-conforming must be converted).

**Septimus's credo:**
> *"Communication is health; communication is happiness."*

He wants to TELL the truth. The doctors want to SILENCE him. The conflict is between expression and suppression.

**The suicide:**
Septimus throws himself from the window as Dr. Holmes enters the room. "I'll give it you!" he cries — his death is the only gift he has left to give. He chooses death over conversion.

## Clarissa's Response to Septimus's Death

> *"Death was defiance. Death was an attempt to communicate; people feeling the impossibility of reaching the centre which, mystically, evaded them; closeness drew apart; rapture faded, one was alone. There was an embrace in death."*

This is Clarissa's internal monologue after hearing about Septimus's death. She has never met him. But she UNDERSTANDS. She recognises his death as a version of what she has felt — the "impossibility of reaching the centre," the fading of rapture, the aloneness.

**Clarissa and Septimus are DOPPELGANGERS** — two versions of the same soul. Septimus dies so Clarissa can live.

> *"He had thrown himself from a window. Up had flashed the ground; through him, blundering, bruising, went the rusty spikes. There he lay with a thud, thud, thud in his brain, and then a suffocation of blackness."*

The physical detail — "rusty spikes," "thud, thud, thud" — is Woolf's way of refusing to aestheticise suicide. It is ugly, painful, physical.

## The Opening Line

> *"Mrs Dalloway said she would buy the flowers herself."*

- Simple, declarative, domestic
- One sentence — but it does enormous work: introduces character, names her by MARRIAGE (not by self), establishes the day, the party, the gesture
- Compare: "I" in The Hours — Daldry's "I'm going to buy the flowers myself" redefines agency
- Woolf begins with third-person naming → identity as socially defined

---

## The Party

The party HAPPENS. The Prime Minister attends. Peter Walsh arrives. Clarissa feels "the terror" and "the ecstasy" simultaneously.

The party is the STRUCTURING DEVICE of the entire novel. The day builds toward it. It gives the woman's life its shape.

Clarissa hears about Septimus's death at the party — and the novel ends with her going back to the party, choosing life over death, choosing ritual against the void.
