## Context Deep Dive

### Woolf's 1925 Context
- **Post-WWI trauma:** A generation of young men dead, a generation of survivors carrying what we now call PTSD. Septimus is Woolf's representation of the war's invisible wounds.
- **Modernism:** Rejection of Victorian realism. Woolf and her Bloomsbury contemporaries believed traditional linear narrative was inadequate to capture consciousness. The mind doesn't move in tidy chapters — so the novel shouldn't either.
- **Women's position:** Clarissa Dalloway cannot have a career, cannot vote (until 1928), cannot express desire for Sally openly. Her life is measured in parties, flowers, and domesticity.
- **Mental illness and the medical establishment:** Septimus's doctors (Holmes, Bradshaw) are the VILLAINS — they impose "proportion" and "conversion" on the non-conforming mind.
- **Queerness encoded not spoken:** Clarissa's love for Sally is a "revelation" but never named as lesbian desire. Septimus's love for his officer Evans is buried even deeper in subtext.

### Daldry's 2002 Context
- **Post-postmodern:** The film assumes its audience knows Woolf. It is metafictional — a film about a woman writing a novel that the audience has likely read.
- **Queerness explicit:** Clarissa Vaughan is openly in a relationship with Sally. Richard is openly gay. Laura's kiss with Kitty is shown, not hidden.
- **AIDS crisis:** Richard's illness replaces shell shock as the era's defining trauma. The wasting body, the stigmatised illness, the lover as caretaker — this is the HIV/AIDS narrative Woolf could not have written.
- **Mental illness destigmatised (partially):** Laura's depression is treated with more compassion than Septimus's psychosis.
- **Three timelines:** Woolf in 1923 (writing), Laura in 1951 (reading), Clarissa in 2001 (living). The novel as virus — passed from writer to reader to the girl named after its heroine.

## Side-by-Side Analysis

### The Opening
| Mrs Dalloway (1925) | The Hours (2002) |
|---------------------|------------------|
| "Mrs Dalloway said she would buy the flowers herself." | "I'm going to buy the flowers myself." |

**Resonance:** Both Clarissas assert agency through the smallest domestic act. The party, the flowers, the ritual — across 80 years, the woman's life is structured by the same gestures.

**Dissonance:** Woolf's third-person naming ("Mrs Dalloway") defines Clarissa by marriage. Daldry's first-person ("I'm going to") gives Clarissa Vaughan direct agency. But does the first person genuinely liberate her? Or is she trapped by the same ritual in a different context?

### The Kiss
| Mrs Dalloway (1925) | The Hours (2002) |
|---------------------|------------------|
| A SINGLE kiss, 34 years ago, encoded as "the most exquisite moment"... wrapped up, never named... violently interrupted by Peter | THREE kisses across three timelines — Virginia/ Vanessa (1923), Laura/Kitty (1951), Clarissa/Sally (2001, ongoing) |

**Resonance:** Both texts recognise queer love as the defining emotional experience of the woman's life.

**Dissonance:** Woolf's kiss is sacred-but-private. Daldry's kisses are visible, ordinary, distributed. Context shapes what can be spoken: 1925 demands encoding; 2002 permits openness.

### The Suicide
| Mrs Dalloway (1925) | The Hours (2002) |
|---------------------|------------------|
| Septimus (stranger, war veteran) dies before Clarissa reaches the party | Richard (lover, AIDS patient) dies at the START of Clarissa's day |

**Resonance:** Both texts use suicide as the STRUCTURAL counterweight to the party — death makes the party meaningful.

**Dissonance:** Woolf's suicide is a POLITICAL act (defiance against medical tyranny). Daldry's is a PERSONAL act (exhaustion, love, letting go). The difference is context: in 1925, doctors were the enemy. In 2002, the body itself is the enemy.

### The Party
| Mrs Dalloway (1925) | The Hours (2002) |
|---------------------|------------------|
| The party HAPPENS. The Prime Minister attends. Clarissa feels terror AND ecstasy simultaneously. | The party DOESN'T happen. Richard kills himself. Clarissa sits with Laura Brown in quiet aftermath. |

**Resonance:** Both texts use the party as the STRUCTURING DEVICE. The day builds toward it. It gives the woman's life its shape.

**Dissonance:** Woolf's Clarissa gets her party. Daldry's Clarissa is denied it. The ritual fails.

### The Older Woman Looking Back
| Mrs Dalloway (1925) | The Hours (2002) |
|---------------------|------------------|
| Clarissa remembers the Sally kiss as "the most exquisite moment of her whole life" — but "the words meant absolutely nothing to her now." The memory is precious but dead. | Laura Brown, in her 80s, visits Clarissa Vaughan after Richard's death. She has survived. She left her family. She chose herself. The film gives the older woman a voice — Laura SPEAKS about her choices. |

**Dissonance:** Woolf's older woman cannot access the feeling of the past. Daldry's older woman CAN talk about the past — but that doesn't mean she is healed.

## The Conversation Reveals

Context shapes what can be spoken. Woolf's kiss is encoded because 1925 demanded encoding. Daldry's kisses are visible because 2002 permits visibility.

**BUT** — and this is key for Band 6 — is visibility liberation? Clarissa Vaughan, in 2001, is still defined by the party, the ritual, the performance. She can kiss Sally openly, but she cannot escape the structure Woolf's Clarissa was trapped in.

**The RESONANCE (shared entrapment) is as powerful as the DISSONANCE (changed context).**

## Essay Structure (Thematic — RECOMMENDED)

**Intro:**
1. Introduce BOTH texts (title, composer, form, year)
2. Define the textual conversation — what does the later text do to/with the earlier one?
3. Thesis — what is revealed by comparing these texts?
4. Outline the shared ideas you'll explore

**Each body paragraph covers ONE shared theme with BOTH texts:**
1. **Topic sentence:** The shared concern + BOTH composers named
2. **Text 1 (Woolf):** Embedded quote → technique → effect → CONTEXT (WHY was this form in 1925?)
3. **Transition:** "Daldry's 2002 reimagining [echoes/transforms/complicates] this through..."
4. **Text 2 (Daldry):** Specific scene/technique → effect → CONTEXT (WHAT can Daldry say that Woolf could not?)
5. **Synthesis:** "The conversation between these texts ultimately reveals..."

**Conclusion:**
1. Reiterate what the textual conversation reveals
2. How context shapes each text's values
3. Final judgment on the significance of the conversation
