# Writing Structures — English Advanced

## Common Mod: Texts & Human Experiences (Paper 1, Section II)

### Basic Structure (TEEL-based paragraph → full essay)

**Intro (≈4 sentences)**
1. Hook — broad statement about the human experience being explored
2. Introduce text: title, author, text type
3. State thesis — your argument about what the text reveals about human experiences
4. Outline — briefly mention the 2-3 ideas/techniques you'll explore

**Body paragraph x3 (≈8-10 sentences each)**
1. **Topic sentence** — idea linking back to thesis (+ technique)
2. **Evidence** — embedded quote (integrated into your sentence)
3. **Analysis** — what does the technique DO? (effect on reader)
4. **Link back** — how does this demonstrate your thesis about human experience?

**Conclusion (≈3-4 sentences)**
1. Restate thesis (reworded)
2. Summarise key ideas
3. Final insight — broader statement about humanity

### Thesis formula
> *[Text] reveals that [human experience] is characterised by [paradox/anomaly], suggesting that [deeper truth about being human].*

Example: *Shakespeare's The Merchant of Venice reveals that the tension between justice and mercy is not a binary choice but a negotiation shaped by prejudice and perspective, ultimately challenging audiences to confront the inconsistencies within their own moral frameworks.*

## Module A — Textual Conversations (Paper 2, Section I)

### Comparative Essay Structure

**Intro**
1. Introduce BOTH texts (title, author/director, text type, year)
2. Define the textual conversation — what does the later text do to/with the earlier one?
3. Thesis — what is revealed by comparing these texts?
4. Outline the shared ideas you'll explore

**Body paragraphs — TWO APPROACHES (pick one):**

**Approach A: Thematic (recommended for Mod A)**
Each paragraph = one shared theme, with BOTH texts discussed:
- Topic sentence: theme + BOTH texts
- Text 1 analysis (quote + technique + effect)
- Text 2 analysis (quote + technique + effect) — show resonance AND dissonance
- Synthesis: what does the conversation between them reveal?

**Approach B: Text-by-text (simpler but weaker)**
- P1-2: Text 1 analysis
- P3-4: Text 2 analysis
- P5: Synthesis paragraph

**Conclusion**
1. Reiterate what the textual conversation reveals
2. How context shapes each text's values
3. Final judgment on the significance of the conversation

### Thesis formula
> *In [later text], [composer] reimagines [earlier text's] exploration of [shared theme] for a [new context], both [resonating with / challenging] its predecessor's values to [new insight].*

Example: *In The Hours, Daldry reimagines Woolf's exploration of female identity for a postmodern context, both resonating with her critique of patriarchal constraints while complicating her modernist pursuit of authentic selfhood through a fragmented, intertextual lens.*

## Module B — Critical Study (Paper 2, Section II)

### Structure
**Intro**
1. Statement about the text's enduring value / textual integrity
2. Your personal critical response to the text
3. Thesis about what the text ultimately reveals
4. Outline

**Body paragraphs**
- Each = one key aspect of the text's textual integrity
- LINK: how does this aspect contribute to the whole?
- Close analysis of language, form, structure
- Refer to critical perspectives (scholars) where relevant

**Conclusion**
- Justify the text's enduring value
- Your final evaluation

### Thesis formula
> *Through [key stylistic feature], [composer] constructs a text of enduring value that compels readers to reconsider [central concern].*

## Module C — Craft of Writing (Paper 2, Section III)

### Creative Piece Structure
1. **Opening** — hook the reader immediately (in medias res, striking image, question)
2. **Rising tension** — build conflict or emotional stakes
3. **Climax / Turning point** — moment of revelation or change
4. **Resolution** — not necessarily happy, but meaningful

**Key: SHOW, DON'T TELL. Use sensory details, dialogue, imagery.**

### Reflection Structure
1. **Purpose** — what was I trying to achieve?
2. **Influences** — how did my prescribed text(s) inspire my choices?
3. **Techniques** — what techniques did I use and why?
4. **Evaluation** — was I successful? What would I change?

### Thesis formula (for reflection)
> *Drawing on [prescribed text]'s use of [technique], I sought to [purpose], crafting [my piece] to [effect on audience].*
