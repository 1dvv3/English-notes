# English Advanced — Key Terms

## General Literary Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Motif | A recurring image, symbol, or idea that develops a theme |
| Allegory | Extended metaphor — a text that can be read on a symbolic level |
| Paradox | A seemingly contradictory statement that reveals a truth |
| Juxtaposition | Placing two elements side by side to highlight contrast |
| Anaphora | Repetition of a word/phrase at the start of successive clauses |
| Imagery | Vivid descriptive language appealing to the senses |
| Tone | The attitude conveyed by the writer towards their subject |
| Voice | The distinctive style/perspective of the author or narrator |
| Structural device | How a text is organised (e.g. framing, fragmentation, flashback) |

## Module-Specific Terminology

### Common Mod — Texts and Human Experiences
| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Human experience | Collective/individual emotions, events, challenges that shape being human |
| Anomaly | Something that deviates from the norm (challenges assumptions) |
| Paradox | Contradictory aspects of human behaviour / the human condition |
| Inconsistency | Contradictions within a character or society |
| Individual vs collective | One person's experience vs shared human experience |
| Emotional response | How the text makes the reader feel — used to evoke empathy/understanding |

### Module A — Textual Conversations
| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Textual conversation | The dialogue between two texts where one reimagines the other |
| Reimagining | Adapting/transforming an original text for a new context |
| Intertextuality | The shaping of a text's meaning by another text |
| Resonance | Where texts echo each other — similarities in themes/ideas |
| Dissonance | Where texts clash — differences due to changing contexts |
| Context | The social, historical, cultural environment of a text |
| Appropriation | Taking elements from a source text and reworking them |
| Values | The beliefs and ideologies embedded in a text |
| Perspective | A particular way of viewing the world |

### Module B — Critical Study of Literature
| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Textual integrity | The unity of a text — how its parts work together to create meaning |
| Enduring value | Why a text remains relevant across time |
| Close reading | Detailed analysis of language, form, structure |
| Personal response | Your own critical interpretation (backed by evidence) |
| Critical reception | How scholars/critics have responded to the text |
| Form | The type/medium of the text (poetry, novel, play, film) |
| Style | The distinctive way a writer uses language |

### Module C — The Craft of Writing
| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Purpose | The reason for writing — what you want to achieve |
| Audience | Who you're writing for — shapes language and tone |
| Form | The structure/shape of your piece (story, speech, reflection, etc.) |
| Style | Your distinctive use of language to create effect |
| Voice | The persona/perspective of your writing |
| Metacognition | Thinking about your own writing process (for the reflection) |
| Reflection | Justifying your creative choices (links to your prescribed texts) |
